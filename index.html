Effect of nitrogen pollution on the hydrophobicity of Echeveria Lola leaves

By: Owen Chen

Background

Leaf hydrophobicity is a trait that varies between plant species, and it can influence various processes of the hydrological cycle and pathogen growth on the leaf. Commercial use of nitrogen fertilizers causes nitrogen pollution, and this study aimed to investigate the effects of nitrogen pollution on the hydrophobicity of Echeveria Lola leaves, a succulent. This study was conducted by growing the plants in controlled environments with varying levels of nitrogen pollution and measuring the water contact angle of the leaves through magnified images and the program ImageJ. The hypothesis that nitrogen pollution affects the hydrophobicity of leaves of Echeveria Lola because it influences the trichome quantity and density of a leaf was supported by the data. The adaxial side of the leaf showed a statistically significant increase in the contact angle after being subjected to different levels of nitrogen. Increasing understanding of the effects of nitrogen on leaf hydrophobicity increases the understanding of how environments can be affected by nitrogen pollution. Leaf wettability has been used in evaluating vegetation hydrological function along with other plant traits.

Rationale

Leaf wettability can be used as a bio-indicator of a habitat’s quality. A high hydrophobicity and low hydrophobicity can have beneficial and harmful benefits on the plant, depending on its environment. High leaf water repellency can help plants survive through prolonged rainfalls. It also has a significant role in transpiration and water interception processes. Knowing the hydrophobicity of a plant can help you determine the appropriate plant for vegetative restoration in certain environments. There are many more effects of hydrophobicity related to a plant’s growth in a certain environment, so it is extremely helpful to understand more about the factors that affect it. Understanding the effects of nitrogen pollution on the hydrophobicity of a plant allows us to understand how beneficial or harmful nitrogen may be to an ecosystem.
